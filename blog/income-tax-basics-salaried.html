Income Tax Basics for Salaried Employees (FY 2025-26)

Everything you need to know about your salary taxation

Last Updated: January 2025 | Reading Time: 8 minutes

Key Update: From FY 2024-25 onwards, the New Tax Regime is the default regime. You must explicitly opt for the Old Regime if you want to claim deductions like 80C, HRA, etc.

How Is Salary Income Taxed?

Your salary has multiple components, and each is taxed differently:

  • Basic Salary: Fully taxable
  • HRA: Partially or fully exempt (Old Regime only)
  • Special Allowance: Fully taxable
  • LTA (Leave Travel Allowance): Exempt with conditions
  • PF Contribution: Employer's share exempt up to 12% of basic
  • Professional Tax: Deductible from salary

New Regime vs Old Regime: Which to Choose?

Quick Rule of Thumb: If your total deductions (80C + 80D + HRA + Home Loan) exceed Rs 3.75 lakh, the Old Regime may save you more tax. Otherwise, stick with the New Regime.

Choose New Regime If:

  • You have minimal investments and deductions
  • Your salary is below Rs 12 lakh (effectively zero tax with rebate)
  • You prefer simplicity without tracking deduction proofs

Choose Old Regime If:

  • You have a home loan with significant interest payment
  • You receive substantial HRA and live in a rented house
  • You maximize 80C, 80D, and NPS deductions

HRA Exemption Calculation

HRA exemption is the minimum of:

  1. Actual HRA received
  2. 50% of Basic Salary (metro cities) or 40% (non-metro)
  3. Rent paid minus 10% of Basic Salary
Important: HRA exemption is only available under the Old Tax Regime. If your annual rent exceeds Rs 1 lakh, you must provide your landlord's PAN to your employer.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Not comparing both regimes: Always calculate tax under both before choosing
  2. Missing investment proof deadline: Submit proofs to employer by January
  3. Ignoring Form 26AS: Always verify TDS credits before filing ITR
  4. Not filing ITR: Even if zero tax, filing helps with loan applications and visa
  5. Last-minute investments: Don't rush investments in March just for tax saving
